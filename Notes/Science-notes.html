Scientific Learning

  • Studies natural objects in a planned & logical way.

  • Investigates phenomena using the scientific method.

  • Variable: A factor influencing an event or phenomenon.

Types of Variables

Type Definition Example (Activity 1.1)
Independent The factor manipulated by the researcher. Extension of rubber band
Dependent The factor affected by changes in the independent variable. Distance traveled by bullet
Controlled Factors kept constant to ensure validity. Thickness of rubber band, size of paper bullet

Difference between them:

Aspect Independent Variable Dependent Variable Controlled Variable
Definition Changed by researcher Affected by independent variable Kept constant
Role Causes change Responds to change Ensures accuracy
Example Sunlight for plants Plant growth Water, soil, pot size
Graph Placement X-axis Y-axis Not plotted

Things to Consider About Variables

  • Only one independent variable per experiment.

  • Only one dependent variable per experiment.

  • Other variables should be controlled.

  • In equations:

    • Dependent variable → Left side.

    • Independent variable → Right side.

  • In graphs:

    • Dependent variable → Y-axis.

    • Independent variable → X-axis.

Units in Science

Fundamental Units

  • Independent units that cannot be broken down further.

  • Examples: kg, m, s, K, mol, cd, A

Derived Units

  • Formed from fundamental units via formulas.

  • Examples: N, Pa, J, W, Hz

Checking Equation Validity

  • Compare units on both sides of an equation.

  • If units match → Valid equation ✅.

  • If units do not match → Invalid equation ❌.

Example

  • Validation of s = v × t:

    • Left side: s → m

    • Right side: v × t → (m/s × s) = m

    • Units match → Valid equation ✅

Key Takeaways

  • Scientific method ensures reliable conclusions.

  • Variables must be identified and controlled.

  • Fundamental units form the base for measurements.

  • Derived units help simplify complex measurements.

  • Equation validation ensures mathematical accuracy.

Evolution & Classification

Evolution refers to the gradual changes leading to the development of complex organisms from simpler forms.

  • All five kingdoms evolved from a common ancestor (living cell).
  • Prokaryotes (Monera) evolved first.
  • Eukaryotes (Protista) evolved later, followed by multicellular organisms.
  • More advanced animals developed from less-developed ones (e.g., Pisces → Reptilia → Mammalia).

Summary

  • 📌 Living beings are classified into five kingdoms based on cell structure, body structure, nutrition, and presence of a cell wall.
  • 📌 Plantae includes autotrophic organisms classified into Algae, Bryophyta, Tracheophyta, Gymnosperms, and Angiosperms.
  • 📌 Animalia includes heterotrophic organisms, further divided into invertebrates and vertebrates.
  • 📌 Evolution and classification are interconnected, showing how organisms have gradually developed new traits over time.

Honey Bee

Introduction

Bees are social insects belonging to Phylum Arthropoda.

Live in hives with three types:

  • Queen bee (largest, lays eggs, controls colony).
  • Drone bee (fertilizes queen, lazy, haploid).
  • Worker bee (collects nectar, builds hive, protects colony).

Bee Colony Organization

  • Highly disciplined and hardworking.

  • Division of labor among bees.

  • Humans practice apiculture (commercial beekeeping).

  • Study of bees is called melittology.

Bee Structure

Body divided into head, thorax, and abdomen.

Features:

  • Compound eyes (see without head rotation).
  • Antennae (detects surroundings).
  • Jointed legs (pollen basket in worker bees).
  • Wings (help in long-distance flight).

Heredity

This note provides a concise summary of heredity, cell division, genetic material, and genetic technology as presented in the document.

Heredity: The Transmission of Characters

Heredity is the phenomenon where parental characteristics are transferred to their offspring. This ensures that offspring resemble their parents, continuing the generation. These traits are transmitted due to genes located on chromosomes within the cell nucleus. Each gene carries specific instructions for a character, enabling the transfer of qualities from parents to children. The study of genes, heredity, and variation is known as genetics. Genetic engineering and medical genetics are sub-branches of this field.

Cell Division: Growth, Reproduction, and Genetic Stability

Organisms increase their cell count through cell division. There are two primary types of cell division: Mitosis and Meiosis.

Mitosis (Equational Division)

Mitotic cell division occurs in all body cells, excluding reproductive cells. A mother cell divides into two identical daughter cells. Each daughter cell is diploid (2n), containing two sets of chromosomes—one paternal and one maternal. The number of chromosomes remains unchanged, hence it is called equational division.

Phases of Mitosis: Mitosis involves two main phases: karyokinesis (division of the nucleus) followed by cytokinesis (division of the cytoplasm).

Significance of Mitotic Cell Division:

  • Physical Growth: Increases cell number with the same genetic makeup.
  • Tissue Repair: Regenerates cells in injured areas, restoring original state.
  • Asexual Reproduction: Facilitates asexual reproduction in some plants and invertebrates.
  • Genetic Stability: Maintains genetic stability across cell generations.

Meiosis (Reductional Division)

Meiotic cell division is confined to the mother cells of major reproductive organs (testis and ovary). In this process, one diploid mother cell divides to form four haploid (n) daughter cells, also known as gametes. The chromosome number is reduced by half in the daughter cells.

Phases of Meiosis: Meiosis occurs in two phases:

  • First Phase: Exchange of genetic material (crossing over) occurs between non-sister chromosomes, leading to variation in genetic makeup. Karyokinesis (nucleus divides into two haploid nuclei) is followed by cytokinesis, forming two haploid cells.
  • Second Phase: Mitotic cell division of these haploid cells occurs through karyokinesis and then cytokinesis, resulting in four cells.

Significance of Meiotic Cell Division:

  • Sexual Reproduction: Plays a crucial role in the sexual reproduction of organisms by forming gametes.
  • Evolution and Variation: Introduces genetic variation, contributing to evolution.
  • Chromosomal Disorder Repair: Helps in repairing chromosomal disorders.

Comparison of Mitosis and Meiosis:

Feature Mitosis Meiosis
Location All somatic cells Reproductive cells (testes and ovaries)
Daughter Cells 2 4
Chromosome Number Remains same (diploid, 2n) Reduced by half (haploid, n)
Genetic Variation No Yes, due to crossing over
Purpose Growth, development, repair, asexual reproduction Sexual reproduction, formation of gametes, introduces variation
Other names Equational division Reductional cell division

Genetic Material: DNA and RNA

Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA)

  • DNA is a long, thread-like structure found inside the cell that carries genetic information.
  • It is present in the cytoplasm of prokaryotic cells and the chromosomes of eukaryotic cells.
  • Viral DNA is enclosed by a protein coat (capsid).

Components of DNA

  • Nitrogenous base
  • Deoxyribose sugar
  • Phosphate ion
  • Nucleoside (nitrogenous base + deoxyribose sugar)
  • Nucleotide (nucleoside + phosphate ion)

Base Pairing in DNA

  • Adenine (A) pairs with Thymine (T) via a double bond (A-T)
  • Guanine (G) pairs with Cytosine (C) via a triple bond (G-C)
  • Carries hereditary information from one generation to another.
  • Transmits hereditary characters during cell division.
  • Undergoes transcription to form RNA, which then synthesizes proteins.

Ribonucleic Acid (RNA)

RNA is a polynucleotide, and each nucleotide is formed by a nitrogenous base, ribose sugar, and phosphate. Unlike DNA, RNA is single-stranded. It is primarily found in the cytoplasm and also in chromosomes. In some viruses, RNA acts as the genetic material and is enclosed by a capsid.

Structure of RNA:

RNA contains four types of nitrogenous bases: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), and Uracil (U). Adenine pairs with Uracil (A-U) by a double bond, and Guanine pairs with Cytosine (G-C) by a triple bond.

Types of RNA: There are three main types of RNA:

  • Messenger RNA (mRNA)
  • Transfer RNA (tRNA)
  • Ribosomal RNA (rRNA)

Function of RNA:

  • The primary function of RNA is protein synthesis.

Chromosomes and Genes

Chromosomes

Chromosomes are prominent, short, and thickened structures formed from chromatin fibers during cell division. They are composed of DNA and histone proteins. Chromosomes are responsible for determining the characteristics of living beings.

Structure of Chromosomes:

Each chromosome consists of two arms called chromatids and a constricted region called the centromere, where the two chromatids are joined.

Types of Chromosomes: Based on function, chromosomes are categorized into:

  • Somatic Chromosomes (Autosomes): These determine the physical characteristics of an individual. In a pair of somatic chromosomes, both members have the same morphology.
  • Sex Chromosomes (Heterosomes): These determine the sex of an individual. The structure of each member of a pair of sex chromosomes is different.

Number of Chromosomes:

The number of chromosomes is constant for a particular organism but varies across species. In humans, somatic cells are diploid (2n), containing 46 chromosomes (23 pairs). Of these, 22 pairs are autosomes, and one pair is sex chromosomes. Human gametes (sperm or ovum) are haploid (n), containing 23 chromosomes.

Genes

A gene is the smallest fragment of DNA on a chromosome that codes for or represents a specific character of an organism. Millions of genes exist on a chromosome.

Role of Genes:

  • Genes transmit hereditary characteristics from parents to offspring.
  • They play a role in evolution through processes like mutation and genetic recombination.
  • Genes contain coded instructions for protein synthesis and hereditary character transmission.

Mendel's Laws of Genetics

Gregor Johann Mendel, known as the "father of genetics," performed experiments on pea plants to establish laws of heredity. He studied seven pairs of contrasting characters.

Result when crossing pea plants with contrasting traits.

Reasons for Mendel's Choice of Pea Plants:

  • Bisexual flowers that are naturally self-pollinating, with easy cross-pollination if needed.
  • Short life cycle allowing faster offspring production.
  • Possession of many contrasting characters.
  • Ability to produce many seeds at once, yielding numerous offspring.
  • Ease of cultivation.

Mendel's Experiments and Observations: Mendel crossed pure tall pea plants with pure dwarf pea plants. The first filial (F1) generation consisted of all tall plants, showing that the tall character was dominant and dwarf was recessive. When F1 hybrids were self-pollinated, the second filial (F2) generation produced both tall and dwarf plants in a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (75% tall, 25% dwarf). The genotypic ratio was 1:2:1 (pure tall: hybrid tall: pure dwarf).

Key Definitions:

  • Dominant Character: The character expressed in the F1 generation when two pure individuals with contrasting characters are crossed.
  • Recessive Character: The character that remains hidden in the F1 generation.
  • Phenotypic Character: Externally visible traits of an organism.
  • Genotypic Character: The genetic constitution or makeup of an organism.
  • Monohybrid Cross: A cross considering one pair of contrasting characters.
  • Dihybrid Cross: A cross considering two pairs of contrasting characters.

Mendel's Laws:

  • Law of Dominance: When a cross is made between two pure individuals with contrasting characters, only one character (dominant) is expressed externally in the F1 generation, while the other (recessive) remains hidden.
  • Law of Purity of Gametes (Law of Segregation): Despite coexisting in a hybrid, two different characters remain pure without losing their originality. During gamete formation through meiosis, the genes of alleles separate, ensuring that only pure characters enter each gamete.

Genetic Technology and its Applications

Genetic technology involves modifying genes by understanding genetic expression and transferring genes to new organisms, utilizing natural genetic variation.

Genetic Engineering: This is a process within genetic technology that alters the DNA structure of an organism using laboratory techniques. It's also known as genetic modification. New traits can be developed by changing a single nitrogen base pair, or by deleting or adding a gene in the DNA. These gene variants can be passed to subsequent generations.

Applications of Genetic Technology:

  • Agriculture: Widely used in agricultural production through molecular markers, transgenic technology, and gene expression to improve yield, quality, and reproductive capacity, and reduce losses.
  • Forensic Science: DNA technology is extensively used in forensic science for criminal investigations and paternity testing, often referred to as DNA testing. DNA testing is a powerful scientific method for establishing facts but requires careful sample collection and transportation to prevent inaccuracies due to its complex and sensitive nature.

Sex Determination in Humans

The sex of an individual is determined by sex chromosomes (heterosomes). Human body cells contain 23 pairs of chromosomes: 22 pairs of autosomes and one pair of sex chromosomes. Males have XY sex chromosomes, while females have XX sex chromosomes.

Process of Sex Determination:

During the reproductive phase, germ cells (diploid, 2n=46) in male testes and female ovaries undergo meiotic cell division to produce haploid gametes (n=23). Male reproductive organs produce sperm with either 22+X or 22+Y chromosomes. Female reproductive organs produce ova with only 22+X chromosomes.

Fertilization:

  • If a sperm with 22+X chromosomes fuses with an ovum (22+X), the offspring will be female (44+XX).
  • If a sperm with 22+Y chromosomes fuses with an ovum (22+X), the offspring will be male (44+XY).

The probability of having a son or daughter is 50%, as male sperm carry both X and Y sex chromosomes in equal proportions.

Selective Breeding Methods

Selective breeding uses different techniques to produce organisms with desirable traits:

  1. Inbreeding: Closely related animals are bred to produce offspring with predictable traits. Continuous inbreeding results in purebred animals like Siamese cats and Labrador Retrievers.
  2. Line Breeding: A form of inbreeding using more distant relatives. It reduces health risks seen in purebreds while still improving desired traits.
  3. Self-Pollination: Many plants self-pollinate, producing seeds with some traits identical to the parent. This helps create genetically similar plants.
  4. Cross Breeding: Two unrelated individuals, often from different species of the same genus, are bred to combine desirable traits. Offspring are called hybrids, which usually show improved qualities.

Examples of Hybrids:

  • Liger: Male lion × Female tiger – Larger than both parents.
  • Tigon: Male tiger × Female lion – Smaller than both parents.
  • Beefalo: Buffalo × Bull – Fertile hybrid.
  • Zebroid: Zebra × Horse – Sterile.
  • Mule: Donkey × Horse – Sterile but strong and fast.
  • Pomato: Potato × Tomato – Grows tomatoes above and potatoes below ground.

Advantages of Cross Breeding:

  • Combines traits from different species.
  • Produces stronger, healthier animals and crops.
  • Improves immunity, productivity, and growth.
  • Increases crop yields.

Disadvantages:

  • Poor management may affect breeding policy.
  • Lower market value than purebreds.
  • Limited export potential.
  • Pure breeds may go extinct.
  • Desired traits may not pass to next generations.

Physiological Structure and Life Process

Main Organs

  • Heart: Central pumping organ.
  • Blood Vessels: Network of tubes that carry blood.
  • Blood: Medium that carries oxygen, nutrients, waste, and hormones.

Function

  • Transport oxygen from lungs to cells and CO2 from cells to lungs.
  • Carry nutrients from digestive system to cells.
  • Distribute hormones and regulate body temperature.
  • Remove waste products to excretory organs.

Blood and Its Components

  • Blood is a liquid connective tissue.
  • Volume: 5.5 liters in adults
  • Nature: Alkaline, pH: 7.35-7.45

Structure:

The following table:

Plasma Functions:

  • Maintains water balance.
  • Transports nutrients, enzymes, and hormones.
  • Removes waste.
  • Fibrinogen helps in clotting.
  • Regulates temperature and pH.
  • Becomes serum when fibrinogen is removed.

RBC (Erythrocytes):

  • Contain hemoglobin (iron + globin).
  • Carry oxygen (form oxyhemoglobin) and CO2 (carboxyhemoglobin).
  • Count: 4.5–5 million/mm³
  • Lifespan: 90–120 days
  • Made in bone marrow, destroyed in liver and spleen

Diseases:

  • Anaemia: Low RBC
  • Polycythemia: High RBC

WBC (Leukocytes):

  • Count: 4000–11000/mm³
  • Lifespan: ~2 weeks

Types:

  • Granular: Neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils
  • Non-granular: Lymphocytes, monocytes

Functions:

  • Immunity (fight infection)
  • Produce antibodies

Diseases:

  • Leukemia: Abnormal increase in WBC
  • Leukopenia: Decrease in WBC

Platelets:

  • Count: 2–4 lakh/mm³
  • Lifespan: 2–3 days
  • Help in clotting with fibrinogen

Diseases:

  • Haemophilia: Deficiency leads to continuous bleeding
  • Thrombocytosis: Excess platelets → clot, stroke

Human Heart

  • Triangular, muscular, in thoracic cavity, left-tilted
  • Covered by pericardium, fluid-filled
  • Made of cardiac muscle, size of a fist (~300g)

External Parts:

  • Coronary artery supplies oxygenated blood
  • Coronary sinus brings deoxygenated blood

Motion and Force

1. Introduction to Gravitation

The force that attracts objects towards each other is known as gravitation.

Earth attracts objects towards its center, as seen in:

  • Stones falling from our hands.
  • Fruits dropping from trees.

The Moon and Earth also attract each other. Mars also has gravitational force, which helps spacecraft land safely.

Examples of Gravitation

Example Description
Falling apple Newton observed an apple falling and questioned the cause.
Moon orbiting Earth A result of mutual gravitational force.
Perseverance Rover landing on Mars Mars' gravity pulls the spacecraft safely to the surface.

2. Newton’s Universal Law of Gravitation

Newton’s Observations:

  • Objects fall vertically instead of moving horizontally.
  • Planets, stars, and the Moon appear to be "stuck" in the sky.

Conclusion: Every object in the universe experiences an attractive force known as gravitation.

Law formulated in 1687:

  • The gravitational force between two objects is directly proportional to the product of their masses.
  • The gravitational force is inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

Mathematical Expression

$$F \propto \frac{m_1 m_2}{d^2}$$

$$F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{d^2}$$

Where:

  • F = gravitational force
  • G = universal gravitational constant \(6.67 \times 10^{-11} \text{ N m}^2/\text{kg}^2\)
  • \(m_1, m_2\) = masses of the objects
  • \(d\) = distance between the objects

3. Variation in Gravitational Force

Dependence on Mass:

  • Doubling the mass of one object doubles the force.
  • Doubling the mass of both objects increases the force by four times.

Dependence on Distance:

  • Doubling the distance reduces the force to one-fourth.
  • Halving the distance increases the force by four times.

4. Consequences of Gravitational Force

  • Formation and stability of the solar system.
  • Movement of planets around the Sun.
  • Creation of tides due to Moon’s gravity.
  • Objects staying attached to Earth's surface.
  • Atmospheric retention — gravity holds Earth's atmosphere in place.

5. Gravity and Acceleration Due to Gravity

Definition: The force exerted by planets/satellites on nearby objects is gravity.

Acceleration Due to Gravity (\(g\)):

  • The acceleration experienced by an object falling freely under gravity.
  • SI unit: m/s².
  • Earth's standard value: 9.8 m/s².

Calculation of \(g\):

Using Newton’s second law and gravitational force:

$$g = \frac{GM}{R^2}$$

Where:

  • \(M\) = Mass of the planet
  • \(R\) = Radius of the planet

6. Factors Affecting Acceleration Due to Gravity

Mass of the Planet:

  • More massive planets have higher gravitational pull.
  • Example: Jupiter has higher \(g\) than Earth.

Radius of the Planet:

  • Larger radius decreases \(g\).
  • Example: Jupiter's larger radius reduces the net increase in its \(g\).

7. Free Fall

Definition: A body falling under gravity alone, without resistance.

Examples:

  • Stone falling freely from a height.
  • Astronauts inside spacecraft experience weightlessness.

Feather and Coin Experiment:

  • Inside a vacuum, all objects fall at the same rate.
  • Air resistance affects lighter objects more.

Equations of Motion for Free Fall:

$$v = u + gt$$

$$v^2 = u^2 + 2gh$$

$$h = ut + \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

Where:

  • \(v\) = final velocity
  • \(u\) = initial velocity
  • \(h\) = height of fall
  • \(g\) = acceleration due to gravity
  • \(t\) = time of fall

Galileo’s Experiment at the Leaning Tower of Pisa

Galileo Galilei disproved Aristotle's belief that heavier objects fall faster by dropping two spheres of different masses. Both hit the ground at the same time, proving that acceleration due to gravity is independent of mass.

Pressure

This chapter covers the concepts of pressure exerted by solids, liquids, and gases, the transmission of pressure in fluids (Pascal's Law), various applications of hydraulic machines, and the phenomenon of upthrust and Archimedes' Principle.

Pressure

  • Definition: Pressure is the thrust acting per unit area of an object.
  • Thrust: A force acting perpendicular to the surface of an object.
  • SI Unit:
    • Thrust: Newton (N)
    • Pressure: Pascal (Pa) or Newton per square meter (N/m²)
  • Pressure Exerted by States of Matter:
    • Solids: Exert pressure on surfaces due to their weight.
    • Liquids & Gases (Fluids): Substances that can flow easily. They are called fluids. Like solids, the fluids too exert pressure. Fluid not only exerts pressure on the bottom of the vessel in which it is kept but on all the walls of the vessel.
  • Fluid Pressure: When an object is placed in a fluid, the fluid exerts pressure on its surfaces.

Transmission of Pressure

  • Solids: Molecules are packed tightly and do not move easily. Pressure applied to one side is generally not transmitted throughout the solid object.
  • Liquids: Intermolecular spaces are very small, so liquid molecules cannot be moved closer together by external pressure. Pressure is transmitted uniformly in all directions in a closed vessel. Liquids are incompressible.
  • Gases: Intermolecular spaces are larger than in liquids. Volume can be reduced by external pressure. Pressure is transmitted in compressed gas, but less effectively than in a liquid. For effective transmission, gas needs to be sufficiently compressed.

Pascal's Law

  • Statement: When a force is exerted at a point in an enclosed liquid, the pressure generated is transmitted normally throughout the liquid in all directions.
  • Formulation: Blaise Pascal, a French mathematician, propounded this law.
  • Mathematical Representation:

    In a hydraulic system with two pistons (small piston with area \( A_1 \), force \( F_1 \); large piston with area \( A_2 \), force \( F_2 \)), the pressure is equal:

    \[ \frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2} \]

    This implies:

    \[ F_2 = F_1 \left(\frac{A_2}{A_1}\right) \]

    The term \( \frac{A_2}{A_1} \) is always greater than 1, meaning the force on the larger piston (\( F_2 \)) is magnified.

Applications of Pascal's Law (Hydraulic Machines)

Hydraulic machines are force-multiplying devices based on Pascal's law.

  • Hydraulic Lift:
    • Used to lift heavy objects like cars or adjust dentist chairs.
    • A small force applied to a small piston is amplified to generate a large force on a larger piston, lifting heavy loads.
    • Example: To lift a 1200 kg car with a large piston area of 5 m² and a small piston area of 0.25 m², a force of 588 N is required on the small piston.
  • Hydraulic Brake:
    • Used in vehicles to stop them instantly.
    • A small force on the master cylinder piston transmits pressure through brake oil to the brake cylinder pistons, which apply a large force to the brake shoes/pads, stopping the wheels.
  • Hydraulic Jack:
    • Used to lift heavy vehicles like trucks and buses.
    • It uses pumping and lifting pistons with different cross-sectional areas and one-way valves to magnify force and lift loads.
  • Hydraulic Press:
    • Used to press objects, fit mechanical parts, bend metal sheets, or compress materials like paper, cotton, and straw.
    • Applies a small force to a small piston to generate a large pressing force from a larger piston.

Upthrust

  • Definition: The net upward force by which water or any fluid pushes an object immersed in it.
  • SI Unit: Newton (N)
  • Cause: Liquid exerts normal pressure on an immersed object from all sides. Pressure increases with depth, so the force on the lower surface is greater than on the upper surface, resulting in a net upward force.
  • Factors Affecting Upthrust:
    • Density of Liquid: Upthrust is directly proportional to the density of the liquid.
      • Higher density liquid = more upthrust.
      • Example: Stone weighs less in water than in edible oil because water is denser. Eggs sink in tap water but float in salty water due to higher density of salty water.
    • Volume of Liquid Displaced: Upthrust is directly proportional to the volume of the displaced liquid.
      • More volume displaced = more upthrust.
      • Example: A large plastic bottle experiences more upthrust than a small one.

Archimedes' Principle

  • Statement: When an object is partially or completely immersed in a liquid, the upthrust exerted on it is equal to the weight of the liquid displaced by it.
  • Applicability: True for all fluids (liquids and gases).
  • Mathematical Representation:

    Upthrust (U) = Weight of liquid displaced (W)

    \[ U = mg = V\rho g \] Where:
    • \( V \) = Volume of liquid displaced
    • \( \rho \) = Density of liquid
    • \( g \) = Acceleration due to gravity
  • Verification: The upthrust on an object immersed in water is equal to the weight of the water it displaces.

Floatation

  • Law of Floatation: For an object to float in a liquid, the weight of the object must be equal to the weight of the liquid that it displaces.
  • Sinking and Floating Conditions:
    • Density comparison: If the density of the object is less than the density of the liquid, the object will float. However, density alone is not the sole determinant; the shape of the object also matters.
    • Resultant Force: An object floats when the upthrust cancels out its weight, making the resultant force zero.
  • Examples of Floatation:
    • Iron Block vs. Iron Ship: An iron block sinks because it cannot displace enough water equal to its weight. An iron ship floats because its wide, long, and deep hull allows it to displace a large volume of water, generating upthrust equal to its weight.
    • Boats: As more weight is added to a boat, it sinks deeper, displacing more water and increasing upthrust to support the added weight. If it sinks too deep, it can no longer displace more water and may sink.
    • Submarines: Control their floatation by filling/emptying ballast tanks with water or compressed air to increase/decrease weight and sink/float.
    • Fish: Use swim bladders (air sacs) to adjust their volume and thus upthrust, allowing them to float or sink.
    • Hydrometer: A device used to measure the relative density of liquids. It floats more in higher-density liquids (more upthrust) and sinks more in lower-density liquids. A lactometer is a type of hydrometer used to test milk purity.
  • Floating in Atmosphere: Upthrust also exists in the air, but due to air's lower density, the upthrust is much less compared to water.

Heat

Introduction to Heat

Scientific reasons related to precautions:

Precaution Scientific Reason
Don't put hands over boiling water. Rapid movement and high kinetic energy of water vapor molecules penetrate skin and cause inner burns.
Don't pour hot water into a glass tumbler in winter. Heat cannot transfer quickly to the outer part, causing inner part to expand and create pressure, leading to cracks.
Don't keep a glass bottle full of water in the deep fridge. Water expands upon freezing, increasing volume and cracking the bottle.
Don't take a hot steel plate with bare hands. Steel's temperature increases instantly upon contact with boiling water, causing burns.
  • Molecular Movement: Matter's atoms/molecules are always in motion.
  • Heat Flow: Heat flows from a body at a higher temperature to a body at a lower temperature until thermal equilibrium.

Thermal Energy, Heat, and Temperature

Kinetic Energy & Temperature:

  • Molecules in lukewarm water have greater kinetic energy than in cold water.
  • Molecules in hot water have even higher speed.
  • Temperature: Index of the average kinetic energy of molecules in a substance.
  • SI unit of temperature: Kelvin (K), commonly used: Degree Celsius (°C).

Absolute Zero:

  • Theoretically considered state of zero thermal energy.
  • 0 K = -273.15°C.

Thermal Energy:

  • Sum of the kinetic energy of molecules.
  • Depends on mass and average speed of molecules.
  • Warm water has highest thermal energy, cold water has lowest (for equal mass).

Heat:

  • Amount of thermal energy transferred due to temperature difference.
  • Not stored in objects; only transferred.
  • SI unit: Joule (J).
  • Measured using a calorimeter.

Heat Transfer Methods:

  • Conduction
  • Convection
  • Radiation

Sensation of Heat:

  • Object feels hot if heat enters our body.
  • Object feels cold if heat leaves our body.

Effect of Heat on Volume

  • States of Matter & Molecular Distance:
    • Solid molecules are bound in a pattern, constantly vibrating.
    • When heated, atoms/molecules gain energy, vibrate faster, and move further apart (volume increases).
    • When an object releases thermal energy, temperature decreases, and the object contracts.

Anomalous Expansion of Water

  • Unique Property:
    • Most substances expand when heated.
    • Water's volume decreases when heated from 0°C to 4°C, and increases when cooled from 4°C to 0°C.
    • Water has minimum volume and maximum density at 4°C.
  • Effects:
    • Aquatic Life: In cold places, water at 4°C sinks to the bottom, allowing aquatic organisms to survive under frozen surface water.
    • Pipe Bursting: Water in pipes expands as temperature decreases, exerting pressure, and when it freezes, pipes may burst.

Specific Heat Capacity

  • Definition: The heat required to change the temperature of 1 kg mass of a substance by 1°C.
  • SI Unit: Joule per kilogram per degree Celsius (J/kg°C).
  • Factors Affecting Heat Absorption:
    • Quantity of heat absorbed (Q) is directly proportional to mass (m).
    • Quantity of heat absorbed (Q) is directly proportional to temperature change (ΔT = T₂ - T₁).
  • Heat Equation: Q = ms(T₂ - T₁).
  • Specific Heat Capacity Table:
Substance Specific Heat Capacity (J/kg°C) Substance Specific Heat Capacity (J/kg°C)
Water 4200 Aluminum 884
Ethyl alcohol 2400 Iron 460
Kerosene oil 2010 Copper 385
Ice 2100 Silver 236
Mercury 126 Gold 130

Uses of High Specific Heat Capacity (of Water):

  • Coolant: Water is an effective coolant in car radiators and thermal power stations.
  • Hot Compressor: Hot water bags use water to release heat for a long time.
  • Body Temperature: Water (70-90% of body mass) helps control body temperature.

Example of problem related to specific heat capacity:

Heat Equation:

The quantity of heat (Q) absorbed or released by a substance can be calculated using the following formula:

$$Q = ms\Delta T$$

  • Q = Quantity of heat (in Joules, J)
  • m = Mass of the substance (in kilograms, kg)
  • s = Specific heat capacity of the substance (in J/kg°C)
  • \( \Delta T \) = Change in temperature (in °C or K), where \( \Delta T \) = Final Temperature (\(T_2\)) - Initial Temperature (\(T_1\))

Example Problem:

Calculate the amount of heat required to raise the temperature of 500 g of water from 15°C to 85°C.

Solution:

    • Given:
    • Mass of water (m) = 500 g = 0.5 kg (since 1 kg = 1000 g)
    • Initial temperature (\(T_1\)) = 15°C
    • Final temperature (\(T_2\)) = 85°C
    • Specific heat capacity of water (s) = 4200 J/kg°C (from the table)
    • Change in temperature (\(\Delta T\)) = \(T_2 - T_1\) = 85°C - 15°C = 70°C
    • Using the heat equation:
    • Q = ms\(\Delta T\)
    • Q = 0.5 kg \(\times\) 4200 J/kg°C \(\times\) 70°C
    • Q = 2100 J/°C \(\times\) 70°C
    • Q = 147,000 J
    • Q = 147 kJ (since 1 kJ = 1000 J)

Therefore, 147 kJ of heat is required to raise the temperature of 500 g of water from 15°C to 85°C.

Sea Breeze & Land Breeze:

  • Caused by difference in specific heat capacity between land and sea.
  • Daytime (Sea Breeze): Land heats faster than sea. Warm air rises over land; cold air from sea flows to land.
  • Nighttime (Land Breeze): Land cools faster than sea. Warm air rises over sea; cold air from land flows to sea.

Measurement of Temperature

Types of Thermometers:

  • Liquid in Glass Thermometer:
    • Contains thermometric liquid (e.g., mercury).
    • Working Principle: Heat causes mercury to expand and move up a capillary tube, indicating temperature.
  • Digital Thermometer:
    • Uses a heat-sensitive thermistor.
    • Working Principle: Heat changes thermistor's resistance, altering electric current, which is converted to a digital display.
  • Radiation Thermometer:
    • Measures temperature without contact.
    • Working Principle: Based on the intensity of infrared radiation; sensor focuses radiation, generating electrical signals.

7. Calibration of Thermometer

  • Definition: The process of determining the scale of a thermometer.
  • Fixed Points:
    • Lower Fixed Point: Temperature of melting ice (0°C).
    • Upper Fixed Point: Temperature of steam just above boiling water at atmospheric pressure (100°C).
  • Scales & Fixed Points:

  1. Mark 0°C by immersing the thermometer bulb in melting ice.
  2. Mark 100°C by holding the bulb in steam from boiling water.
  3. Divide the distance between 0°C and 100°C marks into 100 equal parts, each representing 1°C.

Wave

Introduction

A wave is a disturbance or variation that transfers energy progressively from one point to another in a medium, without a net movement of the particles of the medium itself.

Light & Refraction of Light

When light passes between different transparent media, its speed changes. This change causes the light ray to bend (refract).

Key Points:

  • Rarer Medium: Light speed is higher (e.g., air).
  • Denser Medium: Light speed is lower (e.g., water, glass).

Bending Behavior:

  • Air → Water/Glass: Light bends toward the normal.
  • Water/Glass → Air: Light bends away from the normal.

Terminology in Refraction

  • Normal: An imaginary line drawn perpendicular to the interface (boundary) between two media.
  • Incident Ray: The ray of light that approaches and strikes the surface.
  • Angle of Incidence \(i\): The angle between the incident ray and the normal.
  • Refracted Ray: The ray of light that bends as it passes through the interface into a different medium.
  • Angle of Refraction \(r\): The angle between the refracted ray and the normal in the second medium.
  • Emergent Ray: The exiting ray when light passes through a medium like a glass slab.
  • Angle of Emergence: The angle made by the emergent ray with the normal at the exit point.
  • Lateral Shift: The perpendicular distance between the original and actual emergent path.

Snell’s Law:

\(\frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = n \quad \text{or} \quad n = \frac{c}{v}\)

  • \(i\) = angle of incidence
  • \(r\) = angle of refraction
  • \(n\) = refractive index
  • \(c\) = speed of light in vacuum, \(v\) = speed in the medium

Note: A “denser” optical medium slows light more; this doesn't always match physical density.

Critical Angle & Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

Critical Angle:

The angle of incidence inside the denser medium at which the refracted ray is \(90^\circ\).

Total Internal Reflection Conditions:

  • Light must travel from a denser to a rarer medium.
  • The angle of incidence must be greater than the critical angle.

Applications:

  • Optical Fibres: Light trapped using TIR for data transmission.
  • Diamonds: Internal reflection enhances brilliance.
  • Mirages: Hot air layers cause bending, forming illusions.

Dispersion of Light

White light through a prism splits into colors as wavelengths bend differently.

Magnetic Effect of Current

Definition:

The magnetic effect of current is the phenomenon in which a magnetic field is produced around a current-carrying conductor.

Oersted’s Discovery:

Hans Christian Oersted discovered in 1820 that a magnetic needle placed near a current-carrying wire deflected, proving that electric current produces a magnetic field.

Maxwell’s Right-Hand Thumb Rule:

If a straight current-carrying wire is held with the right hand so that the thumb points in the direction of the current, the curled fingers show the direction of the magnetic field around the wire.

Magnetic Field Around a Solenoid

A solenoid is a cylindrical coil of wire.

The magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform and resembles that of a bar magnet.

When the current direction is reversed, the poles of the magnetic field also reverse.

Right-Hand Grip Rule:

If you hold the solenoid with your right hand such that your fingers follow the direction of current in the coil, then your thumb points to the north pole of the magnetic field.

Factors Affecting Magnetic Field Strength:

  • Magnitude of current
  • Number of turns in the coil
  • Core material (e.g., soft iron core)

Electromagnet

An electromagnet is a temporary magnet made by passing current through a solenoid.

It is used in electric bells, cranes, and motors.

Magnetic Flux

Definition:

Magnetic flux is the total number of magnetic field lines passing through a surface in a magnetic field.

It is denoted by the Greek letter \( \Phi \) and measured in webers (Wb).

Denser magnetic field \( \rightarrow \) higher magnetic flux.

Magnetic flux is stronger at the poles of a bar magnet than at the center.

Motor Effect

Definition:

The motor effect is the phenomenon where a current-carrying wire placed in a magnetic field experiences a force, resulting in motion.

Used in: fans, pumps, electric motors

Working: Coil rotates due to interaction of its field with permanent magnets.

Electromagnetic Induction

Definition:

Electromagnetic induction is the process by which an electromotive force (e.m.f.) is produced in a conductor due to a change in magnetic flux linked with it.

Achieved by: Moving a magnet through a coil or rotating a coil in a magnetic field.

Converts mechanical energy into electrical energy.

Faraday’s Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday’s Law:

When there is relative motion between a conductor and a magnetic field, an electromotive force (e.m.f.) is induced in the conductor. The magnitude of the induced e.m.f. is directly proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux linked with the conductor.

A transformer is a device that increases or decreases the voltage of an alternating current using the principle of mutual induction.

Origin Theories of the Universe

  • Universe started from an explosion of a single dense atom.

  • Supports Big Bang theory.
  • Universe’s size is increasing; expansion is slow but continuous.
  • Gravity slows galaxy velocity but expansion continues.

Future of the Universe

  • Future depends on whether velocity or gravity stops first.
  • Gravity depends on density = mass/volume.
  • Critical density = density needed to stop expansion.

Classification of Elements

These notes provide a structured overview of the classification of elements, the periodic table, and periodic trends.

1. Classification of Elements

  • Pure and impure matters include elements, compounds, and mixtures.
  • 118 elements have been discovered: 92 natural, 26 artificial.
  • Elements with similar properties are grouped together; different properties are in different groups.
  • Classification simplifies the study of elements.

2. Periodic Table

The periodic table is a scientific table for the study of elements. Elements with similar properties are grouped together, and elements with different properties are separated.

Characteristics of the Modern Periodic Table

  • 7 periods and 18 groups.
  • Elements are arranged by increasing atomic number.
  • Elements in the same period have the same number of valence shells.
  • Divided into 18 groups according to the IUPAC system.
  • Metals are on the left, non-metals on the right, and metalloids in the middle.
  • Specific groups:
    • Alkali metals: Group IA (except boron)
    • Alkaline earth metals: Group IIA
    • Transition metals: Groups IIIB to IIB (10 columns of group VIIIB)
    • Halogens: Group VIIA (17)
    • Inert gases/noble gases: Group 0 (18)
    • Lanthanides (atomic number 57-71) and Actinides (atomic number 89-103) are placed below the main block.
  • Elements are classified into s, p, d, or f blocks based on sub-shell electronic configuration.

Electronic Configuration based on Sub-shells

  • A shell is divided into one or more subshells where electrons revolve around the nucleus.
  • Subshells:
    • K shell (n=1): 1s
    • L shell (n=2): 2s, 2p
    • M shell (n=3): 3s, 3p, 3d
    • N shell (n=4): 4s, 4p, 4d, 4f
  • Maximum electrons: s=2, p=6, d=10, f=14.
  • Electron filling follows Aufbau's principle (increasing energy order).
  • Increasing energy order of subshells: 1s < 2s < 2p < 3s < 3p < 4s < 3d < 4p < 5s < 4d < 5p < 6s < 4f < 5d < 6p....

3. Metals, Nonmetals, and Metalloids

Elements are classified into three groups based on their metallic characteristics.

Metals

  • Left side of the modern periodic table.
  • All elements of Group IA (except boron) and Group IIA are metals.
  • Groups IIIB to IIB are less reactive transition metals.
  • Generally good conductors of heat and electricity.
  • Group IA (Li, Na, K) are reactive alkali metals, forming strong bases in water.
    • Valence shell electronic configuration: ns¹.
    • Soft with low density.
  • Group IIA (Mg, Ca) are alkaline earth metals, found as soluble oxides on Earth's surface.
    • Valence shell electronic configuration: ns².

Non-metals

  • Right side of the periodic table.
  • Includes Group VA, VIA, VIIA, and Group VIIIA (18 or 0).
  • Group VIIA (17) elements (F, Cl, Br, I) have seven valence electrons.
    • Outermost electronic configuration: ns²np⁵.
    • Easily gain one electron.
    • Most reactive nonmetals.
    • Soft with low density.
  • Group 0 (18) elements (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn) have eight valence electrons (He has two).
    • Valence shell electronic configuration: ns²np⁶.
    • Also known as noble gases or inert gases.
    • Do not participate in chemical reactions.

Metalloids

  • Located between metals and nonmetals.
  • Exhibit properties of both metals and nonmetals.
  • Poor/semiconductors of electricity.
  • Examples: Silicon (Si), Germanium (Ge), Bismuth (Bi).

Element properties depend on their group and period. The periodic table is essential for studying these properties.

These notes provide a structured overview of chemical reactions, their types, and factors affecting their rate.

1. What is a Chemical Reaction?

  • A process involving a chemical change.
  • Involves the loss or gain or sharing of electrons between reactants or the exchange of their ions to form products.
  • Reactants change to form products.

2. Chemical Equation

The representation of a chemical reaction in the form of words, symbols, or chemical formula.

4. Rate of Chemical Reaction

  • The change of reactants to products per unit time.
  • Different chemical reactions have different rates (some are fast, some are slow).

Pressure:

  • Affects the rate of reaction for gases.
  • Rate of reaction increases with increasing pressure for gaseous reactants.

Surface Area:

  • If the surface area of contact of reactants is more, the rate of reaction is also more.

Light:

  • Some chemical reactions need light.
  • The presence of light increases the rate of reaction.
  • Examples include photosynthesis and the decomposition of silver nitrate.

  • Negative impacts on human health.

  • CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid)

Effects of Acid Rain

  • Fades monuments (temples, buildings, statues made from marble).
  • Equation: CaCO₃(s) + H₂SO₄(aq) → CaSO₄(aq) + CO₂(g) + H₂O(l).
  • Increases acidity of soil (decreasing productivity).
  • Affects aquatic animals.
  • Causes skin diseases in humans.
  • Causes negative effects on human health.

Methods to Prevent Acid Rain

  • Less production of oxides of nitrogen and sulphur.
  • Use renewable sources of energy in place of fossil fuel.
  • Awareness raising.

Metal and Non-metals

Matter is classified into pure and impure substances. Elements and compounds are pure substances. Elements are further classified into metals, non-metals, and metalloids.

1. Minerals

  • Definition: Elements and compounds found naturally in the Earth's crust. They are sources of metals.
  • Composition: Earth's crust is primarily silicate minerals (~90%). Sulphide, oxide, carbonate, sulphate, and phosphate ores are also present.
  • Nature: Most minerals are inorganic, pure, solid, and crystalline, possessing a fixed chemical formula.
  • States:
    • Found as compounds in the Earth's crust, with some exceptions.
    • Reactive metals (e.g., sodium, potassium, calcium, iron) are found as compounds.
    • Non-reactive metals (e.g., gold, silver) exist in their pure state.
  • Examples & Locations in Nepal: Hematite, cuprite, granite, limestone, talc, red clay, and coal are found in various regions including Dang, Salyan, Rolpa, Gulmi, Pyuthan, Lalitpur, Palpa, and Nawalparasi.

2. Ore

  • Definition: Minerals from which metals can be extracted in large quantities, easily, and economically with low cost.
  • Relationship with Minerals: All ores are minerals, but not all minerals are ores. Some minerals contain less metal or are difficult/expensive to extract.
  • Mining: The process of extracting ores and other useful substances/minerals from the Earth's crust by digging.

4. Metallurgy

  • Definition: The science dealing with the properties, production, and purification of metals. It involves the stepwise extraction process of metals from their ores.
  • General Steps (5 main steps):
    1. Grinding: Crushing ores into small particles using rollers/machines.
    2. Concentration: Removing impurities (gangue like mud, sand, rocks) from the ore to increase the percentage of metal.
      • Methods depend on properties of ore/impurities: Hydraulic/gravity separation (density difference), Magnetic separation (if ore or impurity is magnetic), Froth floatation (if ore/impurity is hydrophobic/hydrophilic).
    3. Oxidation: Converting concentrated ores to metal oxides.
      • Roasting: Strong heating in presence of air/oxygen for non-oxide ores (generally sulphide ores). Example: ZnS → ZnO.
      • Calcination: Strong heating in absence of air/passing oxygen for carbonate ores. Example: CaCO₃ → CaO.
    4. Reduction: Removing oxygen from metal oxide to get the metal.
      • Metal oxides are treated with reducing agents (carbon, coke, carbon monoxide, hydrogen).
      • Oxides of very reactive metals (Na, K, Ca, Mg, Al) are very stable and reduced only by electrolysis.
      • Zinc and mercury oxides are reduced by carbon.
      • Silver and mercury oxides are unstable and reduced by heat.
      • Smelting: Heating metals beyond their melting point with a reducing agent, where impurities separate as gases or slag.
    5. Refining: Obtaining metals in a pure state after reduction by removing remaining impurities.
      • Methods include distillation and electro refining.
      • Distillation: Impurities removed by boiling, used for metals/impurities with low boiling points (e.g., mercury).
      • Electro-refining: Obtaining ~99% pure metals (e.g., iron, silver, copper, gold) using electrolysis in a voltameter.
        • Impure metal is connected to the anode (positive terminal).
        • Pure metal is connected to the cathode (negative terminal).
        • A salt solution of the metal is used as an electrolyte.
        • The impure metal at the anode erodes, and pure metal is deposited on the cathode.

Hydrocarbon and its Compounds

Compounds are classified as carbonic (organic) or non-carbonic (inorganic). Inorganic compounds come from minerals, except for carbon oxides, carbonates, bicarbonates, and carbides. Organic compounds are obtained from plant and animal sources. All carbon compounds, except its oxides, carbonates, bicarbonates, and carbides, are organic. Combustible substances from plants and animals are also organic compounds.

1. What are Hydrocarbons?

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds composed solely of carbon and hydrogen. Petroleum is their primary source.

2. Organic Compounds (General)

These are typically carbon compounds covalently bonded to other carbon or hydrogen atoms. They can also contain elements like oxygen, nitrogen, halogens, sulfur, and phosphorus. Examples include methane, ethane, ethene, acetylene, methanol, chloroform, urea, insulin, protein, and oil. The study of these compounds is called carbonic chemistry or organic chemistry.

3. Classification of Hydrocarbons

Hydrocarbons are classified as saturated or unsaturated based on the types of bonds between carbon atoms.

Saturated Hydrocarbons (Alkanes)

  • Have a single covalent bond between carbon atoms.
  • Very stable and less reactive.
  • Also known as paraffin or alkane.
  • General formula: CᵢH₂ᵢ₊₂ (where 'n' is the number of carbon atoms).

  • Have double or triple covalent bonds between carbon atoms.
  • Less stable and very reactive.
  • Also known as olefins.

4. Hydrogenation

The process of making Vanaspati ghee from vegetable oil. Unsaturated fatty acids change to saturated fatty acids. Example: Alkene + H₂ → Alkane.

5. Homologous Series

A series of hydrocarbons represented by the same general formula. Adjacent members differ by a CH₂ group , and their molecular weight differs by 14. Examples for alcohols include Methanol (CH₃OH), Ethanol (C₂H₅OH), and Propanol (CH₃CH₂CH₂OH).

6. Alkyl Radical

A group formed by removing one hydrogen atom from an alkane molecule. Represented by the general formula CᵢH₂ᵢ₊₁-. Examples: -CH₃ (Methyl), -C₂H₅ (Ethyl).

7. Functional Group

An atom or group of atoms that determines the structure and chemical reactivity of an organic compound. Examples: -OH, -CHO, -COOH.

8. Nomenclature of Hydrocarbons (IUPAC)

9. Important Hydrocarbons and Alcohols

Methane (CH₄)

  • Found in marshy places (marsh gas), gobar/bio gas, sewage gases, and mineral oil.
  • Colourless, odourless, tasteless.
  • Uses: Cooking fuel (gobar/biogas), industrial fuel, making carbon black (for printing ink, shoe polish, paint), preparing chloroform, carbon tetrachloride, methyl alcohol, formaldehyde, and industrial preparation of hydrogen gas.

Ethane (C₂H₆)

  • Saturated hydrocarbon with two carbons and a single bond.
  • Found in natural gas, coal gas, and petroleum mines.
  • Colourless, odourless, tasteless, insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents.
  • Uses: Produces heat on combustion (for metal welding), preparing carbonic compounds (ethyl chloride, nitro ethane).

Propane (C₃H₈)

  • Saturated hydrocarbon with three carbons.
  • Found in natural gas and petroleum mines.
  • Colourless, odourless, tasteless, insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents.
  • Uses: Highly flammable gas (fuel), making carbonic compounds, coolant in petroleum industries.

Butane (C₄H₁₀)

  • Saturated hydrocarbon with four carbons.
  • Found in natural gas and petroleum mines.
  • Colourless, odourless, tasteless, insoluble in water, soluble in organic solvents.
  • Uses: Raw material for synthetic rubber, mixed with methane in LPG (fuel).

Alcohol

  • Compounds formed by replacing one or more hydrogen atoms of alkanes with a -OH group.
  • The -OH group is the functional group of alcohols.
  • General formula: CᵢH₂ᵢ₊₁OH.
  • Classified by the number of -OH groups:
    • Monohydric alcohol: One -OH group (e.g., Methyl alcohol, Ethyl alcohol).
    • Dihydric alcohol: Two -OH groups (e.g., Glycol).
    • Trihydric alcohol: Three -OH groups (e.g., Glycerol).

Important Alcohol Compounds and Uses

Methyl Alcohol (Methanol, CH₃OH)

  • Monohydric alcohol.
  • Uses: Prepare methylated spirit solvent (paints, varnishes), prepare perfumes, paints, medicines, synthetic clothes, manufacture formaldehyde, dry cleaning.

Ethyl Alcohol (Ethanol, C₂H₅OH)

  • Monohydric alcohol. Generally refers to alcohol in daily life.
  • Colourless liquid, soluble in water.
  • Uses: Prepare hard drinks (whisky, wine, beer), sterilize syringes/wounds in hospitals/health posts/nursing homes, preserve biological specimens, thermometers, manufacture of polyethylene, terylene, soaps, paints, dyes, organic solvent.

Glycol (Ethane-1,2-diol, C₂H₄(OH)₂)

  • Dihydric alcohol. Two -OH groups attached to each carbon of hydrocarbons.
  • Used in industrial and food stuff.

Glycerol (Glycerine, Propane-1,2,3-triol, C₃H₅(OH)₃)

  • Trihydric alcohol.
  • Formed by replacing three hydrogens of propane with three -OH groups.
  • Greek word 'glykeros' meaning sweet.
  • Colourless, sweet viscous liquid.
  • Soluble in water/alcohol, insoluble in ether.
  • Uses: Protect skin by trapping moisture (prevent dryness), sweetening agent in food, make good quality soap/lotion/cosmetics/shaving creams, keep tobacco moist, prevent dryness of fruits/foods, prevent them from decaying.

Chemicals used in Daily Life

Chemicals are used daily for various purposes like cleaning, food preservation, and as pesticides or fertilizers. Industrial chemistry studies the transformation of matter to manufacture useful materials through chemical processes.

1. Food Preservatives

  • Definition: Substances used to protect food from decaying due to bacteria, yeast, mold, etc. They prevent spoilage.
  • How they work: They check the growth of microbes in food.
  • Classification:
    • First Class Preservatives: Natural sources, used in necessary amounts.
      • Examples: Sugar, salt, vinegar, honey, spices, edible oils. Salt and oil in pickle preserve it.
    • Second Class Preservatives: Chemical derivation, used in limited amounts.
      • Examples: Sodium or potassium nitrate, benzoic acid and its salts, sulphur dioxide.
  • Caution: Should not use more than one type in food. Chemical preservatives can be harmful. Use natural ones as much as possible.

2. Fruit Ripening Chemicals

  • Chemical Used: Calcium carbide is often used excessively as it is easier and cheaper.
  • Caution: Be very careful when consuming fruits ripened with this.
  • Health Problems (Overuse): Skin irritation, burning, blisters, lung effusion, eye irritation.

4. Pesticides

  • Definition: Poisonous substances to remove, destroy, kill, and control harmful pests. Pests include insects, plants, animals, etc. Defined by 'Pesticide Management Act 2076 (B.S.)'.
  • Use in Nepal: Average 396 grams per hectare. ~80% mix with soil, risking water pollution.
  • Impacts: Kill harmful and useful insects. Cause ecosystem imbalance. Polluted water affects animals. Affects reproductive/respiratory systems.

Classification of Pesticides

Based on Pests Affected:

  • Insecticides: Kill insects. Examples: Malathion, Cypermethrin, Fenvalerate.
  • Fungicides: Kill fungi. Examples: Dimethomorph, Mancozeb.
  • Herbicides: Destroy unwanted plants. Examples: Butachlor, atrazine.
  • Rodenticides: Kill rats/rodents. Examples: Zinc phosphide, bromadiolone.
  • Miticides: Kill mites. Examples: bifenazate.
  • Fumigants: Kill pests in storage with poisonous gases. Example: Aluminium phosphide.

Basis of Mode of Action:

  • Contact: Kill on direct contact. Examples: Malathion, Chlorpyrifos.
  • Internal: Pests eat plant parts with pesticide and are killed. Examples: Malathion, Cypermethrin, Fenvalerate.
  • Systemic: Absorbed by plant parts, making plant poisonous. Examples: Thiamethoxam, Dinotefuran.
  • Fumigant: (See above definition).

5. Chemical Pollution

  • Problem: Causes trouble for survival of humans and living beings. Degradation of air, water, soil. Causes ecosystem imbalance.
  • Negative Impacts: Airborne/waterborne diseases. Increased atmospheric CO₂ raises Earth's temperature. Direct impact on all living beings.
  • Sources: Industrial products (cement, glass, ceramics, plastic, fiber), chemical fertilizers, soap, detergent, pesticides.
  • Management:
    • Manage/dispose industrial waste (glass, ceramics, plastic, fiber) properly.
    • Tie/cover cement sacs.
    • Use organic/compost fertilizer instead of chemical.
    • Use chemical fertilizers/pesticides only as expert suggested.
    • Collect wastewater from soap/detergent separately, don't mix directly with water sources/land.
